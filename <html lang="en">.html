Consequences of Using Computers

Computers-it is no wonder that they are so widely used across the world,I mean what can they not do? They allow us to work,connect with people from all across the globe,browse the internet and much more.However, there are some safety concerns when using computers-read below to find out about some example case study which involved legal,ethical and social concerns.

Example Case Study-Google Street View

Although Google Street View is a handy and innovative tool, it does raise a few legal,ethical and cultural concerns:

When Google expanded Street View to show the inside of buildings, several important ethical concerns came up. Privacy was a big issue, especially in private spaces where personal information could be exposed. Getting permission from building owners was crucial. They also needed to make sure the data they collected was safe and not misused. Being respectful of different cultures and avoiding copyright problems were ethical challenges too. Additionally, they had to consider issues like user consent for facial recognition, accessibility, and balancing business interests with public good. Educating users on their rights and responsibilities was also important. Legally, Google faced some key challenges when expanding Street View indoors. They had to ensure they had proper consent or legal rights to capture interior images. Copyright and intellectual property concerns had to be addressed to avoid legal disputes. Adhering to data security and protection laws was vital to prevent legal issues related to data breaches. Defining the legal boundaries between public and private spaces was necessary. And navigating different country-specific laws and regulations when expanding globally was another legal complexity they had to tackle.

Example Case Study-Data Company

Between 2008 and 2010, a company gathering data for an online mapping system, using cars equipped with cameras and WiFi equipment, inadvertently collected personal information transmitted on personal WiFi networks. This situation highlights several key points: such as how data was collected. The data collection occurred because the company’s mapping vehicles were equipped with WiFi-sniffing capabilities, allowing them to capture data from unsecured or poorly secured personal WiFi networks as they drove by. Essentially, these vehicles unintentionally intercepted and stored data packets from these networks. Steps owners of networks could have taken: To prevent such data collection, network owners could have taken several precautions. Firstly, they could have secured their WiFi networks with strong encryption and passwords, making it significantly more difficult for unauthorized devices to intercept their data. Additionally, disabling broadcasting of network names (SSID) and regularly updating router firmware to patch security vulnerabilities could have increased network protection. This also created some legal and ethical issues such as the inadvertent collection of personal data on WiFi networks which raised significant legal and ethical concerns. Legally, this constituted a breach of privacy, possibly violating data protection laws depending on the jurisdiction. Ethically, it was a breach of trust and privacy expectations. Collecting personal data without consent raised questions about user rights and consent in the digital age. Lessons learned and changes in practices: The incident likely taught the company valuable lessons about data collection practices. They may have realized the importance of clear policies and protocols for data handling, ensuring that only authorized and reviewed data collection methods are used. This incident could have prompted them to implement stricter data privacy controls and encryption standards. They might also have adopted more transparent practices, informing users about data collection during mapping activities and providing opt-out options. Ultimately, this incident likely led to a greater emphasis on ethical considerations, user consent, and legal compliance in the company’s data collection practices. In summary, the inadvertent collection of personal WiFi data by a mapping company between 2008 and 2010 revealed the importance of secure network practices, raised legal and ethical concerns, and likely prompted the company to improve its data collection procedures to ensure user privacy and legal compliance.
